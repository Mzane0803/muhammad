There is a question most of us avoid because it seems impractical. It is not. When you say *I*, what do you mean? The body you wash each morning? The continuous stream of thought that disappears every night in sleep? Neither survives a careful look.

The mind-body problem is commonly introduced as a puzzle for professional philosophers. This is wrong. It is a problem about you, right now, reading this. If the thing you call *I* cannot be located, you are living a life on assumptions you have never examined.

> The self is not a thing you have. It is a pattern that holds, and when it holds loosely, you are freer than you thought.

Neuroscience gives us a tidy story: there is no "center" in the brain where experience assembles. Consciousness is not a room; it is a process distributed across billions of cells. And yet the feeling of being someone, of being *here*, behind the eyes, is the most undeniable fact any of us has.

Buddhist practice arrives at the same conclusion from the opposite direction. Sit still long enough and the "I" dissolves into its parts: a sensation here, a thought there, a story tying them together. The tying is the trick. Loosen it and something quieter stays.

Both traditions agree on what follows. If the self is less solid than it feels, then ambition, fear, and identity, *everything* you build on top of it, is also less solid. This is not bad news. It is the first real freedom.

---

I do not think the mind-body problem will be solved in my lifetime. I also do not think that matters. Living as if the question were open, rather than pretending we have the answer, is itself a practice. It changes how you hold a disagreement, how you hold a loss, how you hold yourself at 3 a.m.

The body you have is real. The mind you have is real. Whether they are the same thing is still, thankfully, an open question.
