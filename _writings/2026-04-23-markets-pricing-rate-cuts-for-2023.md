++**Summary**++

Globally, major central banks have continued their hawkish monetary policy, although there is belief in the markets that the end of rapidly increasing interest rates is coming to an end, with inflation increasingly under control. The Fed and the Bank of England started the month with a 75bps hike, on the heels of the Bank of Canada and the ECB closing October with an increase of the same magnitude. All four of those central banks are set to meet again in mid-December, and inflation data published in November is set to provide clarity on the path rates will take in the months to come.

++**Technical Analysis**++ 

Over the last year, inflation control has been the main driver behind the rise in interest rates and the monetary policy tightening. As such, monthly inflation data have proved to be a good indicator of central bank policy. Earlier in November, the US announced that the October CPI reading to have increased at a lower rate than expected, thus suggesting that the Fed will have room to slow its aggressive hikes. This impression was confirmed during a Powell speech on November 30th, when the Fed’s chair announced a likely 50bps hike for the December meeting. Markets nonetheless believe the Fed to maintain the 5% terminal rate target, which is expected to be reached in the spring.

In Canada, YoY inflation was steady at 6.9%, with the core CPI reading, which excludes volatile energy prices, stood at 5%. The index gained 0.7% MoM, below the 0.8% estimate. Further, a stronger than expected jobs report continues to show that the economy is not yet under control. Tiff Macklem has stated that rates will need to increase some more, though the end of the tightening cycle is near.

In the UK, inflation has been highly driven by the energy crisis that resulted from the Ukraine war and the increased demand associated to the start of winter, with gas prices up 36.9% and electricity up 16.9%. In the UK, CPI rose to 11.1% from last year, higher than the BOE’s 10.9% forecast. As such, this suggests that the central bank will likely increase rates further at its December meeting, with markets currently pricing an implied rate increase of 58bps.

In contrast, the Euro-zone inflation has slowed for the first time in 1.5 years, with the November reading standing at 10%, a 0.4% positive surprise. Despite the positive news, ECB President Lagarde announced to the European Parliament that “it would surprise [her]” if price growth had peaked. This promotes the idea that the ECB may not be done with raising rates, with the markets pricing in a 56bps hike for the December meeting.
