# **Introduction: Attention as the Defining Resource**

In 1971, the economist Herbert Simon observed that information *"consumes the attention of its recipients"* and that, therefore, *"a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention"* (Simon 40–41). What Simon identified half a century ago has since become the organising logic of the global economy. Tim Wu, in *The Attention Merchants*, argues that human attention has become the foundational commodity of the modern age  not merely a byproduct of information overload, but the deliberate target of an industry built on capturing and reselling what we look at, for how long, and to whom that looking can be sold (Wu 6–10). The numbers confirm the scale of this capture. The average person now spends nearly seven hours per day on screens, and global digital advertising revenue surpassed one trillion dollars in 2025 (DataReportal; WPP Media). Attention is no longer simply something we pay. It is something that is extracted from us, industrially, and at scale.

This paper argues that live theatre occupies a unique and increasingly urgent position within this landscape. In an era defined by the fragmentation and commodification of attention, the four productions encountered in this course *Primary Trust*, *White Girls in Moccasins*, *The Container*, and *The Tempest*  each stage a different crisis of attention. They reveal how focus is controlled, surrendered, manipulated, and reclaimed. More importantly, they do so within a medium that demands something the digital attention economy cannot: sustained, embodied, collective, unrepeatable presence in a shared room. Theatre, I will argue, does not merely represent attention it restructures it. That restructuring is what makes the medium politically and personally significant in ways that extend well beyond aesthetics.

These figures illustrate the structural environment in which the plays under discussion were experienced. The audience member who sits down in a theatre in 2026 does so after spending, on average, the majority of their waking hours in mediated, algorithm-curated environments. Theatre asks that person to do something radical: to be present, without a device, in a room of strangers, attending to a single unfolding event for which there is no rewind, no scroll, and no skip. The implications of that demand are the subject of what follows.

# **Attention as Weapon: White Girls in Moccasins**

The most structurally explicit staging of the attention economy occurred in Yolanda Bonnell’s *White Girls in Moccasins*, where a game-show format is deployed as both entertainment device and political mechanism. At a critical moment, an Indigenous man is shot and then the game show resumes, almost immediately, with lights, sound, and rhythm flooding the space as though nothing had happened. The audience, which had been laughing moments earlier, begins to laugh again. The system of attention reasserts itself.

This is not simply a dramatic technique. It is a structural reproduction of how the attention economy operates. Wu describes how the foundational business model of attention merchants has always involved offering free diversion in exchange for a moment of the audience’s time, which is then resold to the highest bidder (Wu 6–8). The game show in Bonnell’s play functions identically: it offers spectacle, absorbs violence, and redirects focus before discomfort can settle. Joe Kelleher, in *Theatre & Politics*, argues that politics in theatre is fundamentally about *"the attempt to determine relations of power in a given space"* (Kelleher 8). In this scene, power is exercised not through the act of violence itself but through what follows it the determination of what the audience will attend to next. The game show decides. The audience complies.

What makes this particularly disturbing is the audience’s complicity. The laughter and applause are not neutral behaviours; they are the mechanism by which the system of distraction sustains itself. Kelleher notes that theatre’s political potential lies not in its capacity to deliver messages but in how it affects spectators and what it compels them to feel after the moment passes (Kelleher 54–58). Bonnell’s play exploits this by making the audience the instrument of its own distraction. The spectators do not merely observe the redirection of attention — they perform it. This mirrors the structure of the digital attention economy precisely: on social media, news cycles, and content platforms, the user is simultaneously the audience, the product, and the mechanism of perpetuation.

# **Attention as Intimacy: Primary Trust**

If *White Girls in Moccasins* stages attention as a weapon, Eboni Booth’s *Primary Trust* examines what happens when attention becomes the only form of intimacy available to a person. Kenneth, the play’s protagonist, speaks to an imaginary friend named Bert with a sustained, focused sincerity that he cannot direct toward any real person. Bert receives the quality of attention that genuine human relationships demand but that Kenneth’s isolation has made impossible.

Helen Freshwater, in *Theatre & Audience*, describes how audience members are always individuals who bring their own *"personal histories and immediate preoccupations"* to a performance (Freshwater 5). Kenneth’s relationship with Bert is, at its core, a question about the direction of those preoccupations: to whom do we give our deepest attention, and does it matter whether the recipient is real? In an economy that fragments attention across dozens of daily inputs notifications, feeds, autoplay videos Kenneth’s ability to sustain deep focus on a single, invented relationship is both tragic and uncomfortably aspirational. He gives Bert something that the attention economy has made scarce: unbroken presence.

Freshwater also cites Tim Etchells’s description of theatrical experience as a space where *"we are together and alone"*, aware of both the temporary bonds linking the audience and the persistent separateness between them (Freshwater 6). Kenneth’s condition literalises this paradox. He is with Bert and alone. The audience watches this and recognises something about its own condition the hours spent in parasocial connection with podcasters, streamers, and algorithmically curated feeds. The quality of attention Kenneth gives Bert is not categorically different from the attention millions give to digital presences who will never know they exist. Theatre makes this visible by staging the intimacy of one-directional attention in a space where genuine collective attention is simultaneously occurring.

# **Attention as Politics: The Container**

Clare Bayley’s *The Container* asks a more fundamental question: who receives our attention at all? The play stages a group of migrants locked inside a shipping container, invisible to the world outside. They are, by design, people whom the global order has decided not to look at. Their suffering occurs below the threshold of public attention, and the play’s power lies in forcing a theatre audience to attend to what the broader world refuses to see.

Margaret Werry, in *Theatre & Tourism*, argues that theatre and tourism are *"kindred practices"* that both stage *"experiences of temporary escape to distant places, times, or different lives"* (Werry 1–2). The critical difference, which Werry’s framework helps expose, is between those who move through the world by choice and those who move because staying means death. Tourism is chosen attention directed at chosen experiences; the tourist decides what to look at, for how long, and with what degree of comfort. Migration, as staged in *The Container*, is the inverse: people moving through spaces that refuse to look at them, stripped of the agency to direct anyone’s attention toward their suffering.

This connects directly to the attention economy’s political dimension. The allocation of attention is never neutral. As Simon recognised in 1971, when information is abundant, the scarce resource is not data but the willingness to attend to it (Simon 40–41). The same principle applies to human suffering. The information about migration the routes, the deaths, the exploitation is available. What is scarce is sustained attention to it. News cycles treat migration as a recurring story that appears, generates temporary engagement, and is displaced by the next event. *The Container* refuses this displacement. The audience cannot scroll away. They sit in a dark, confined space and attend to people whose existence the attention economy has rendered invisible. The play’s formal structure its darkness, its claustrophobia, its refusal of spectacle is an act of attentional resistance.

# **Attention as Power: The Tempest**

Shakespeare’s *The Tempest*, in the Stratford Festival production, brings the attention economy’s logic to its most concentrated expression: a single figure who controls what everyone sees, hears, and believes. Prospero is, in essence, an attention merchant. She captures the attention of every character on the island and directs it according to her design. Ferdinand sees Miranda because Prospero arranges it. The court party experiences guilt because Prospero stages it. Ariel performs because Prospero commands it. Nothing on the island escapes Prospero’s editorial control.

Freshwater argues that the relationship between performance and audience is *"indispensable"* to theatre’s function and that audiences are actively shaped by what happens on stage, not merely passive witnesses to it (Freshwater 4–6). In *The Tempest*, this shaping is made explicit. The audience experiences a version of Prospero’s control: we accept the illusion, follow the manipulation, and allow our attention to be directed precisely where the production wants it. We are inside the system, not outside it. In this sense, theatre does not merely represent the attention economy it performs a miniature version of it, with the director and playwright functioning as attention architects.

What makes the play’s ending significant within this framework is that Prospero chooses to relinquish control. She breaks the spell. She releases Ariel. She addresses the audience directly in the Epilogue, asking for their applause which is to say, asking them to give attention voluntarily rather than having it extracted. This is the crucial distinction between theatre and the digital attention economy: theatre ends. It gives your attention back. The play concludes, the lights come up, and the audience re-enters a world of choice. Instagram, TikTok, and the algorithmic feed do not offer this release. They are designed, structurally, never to end. Prospero’s decision to break the spell is a decision that no attention merchant would ever make voluntarily.

# **Conclusion: Theatre as Counter-Model**

The four productions examined here stage four distinct relationships to attention: weaponised distraction in *White Girls in Moccasins*, invented intimacy in *Primary Trust*, political invisibility in *The Container*, and totalised control in *The Tempest*. Read together through the lens of the attention economy, they reveal a consistent insight: attention is never neutral. It is always allocated, directed, captured, or withheld, and the forces that determine its flow are political, economic, and deeply personal.

Live theatre operates as a counter-model to the attention economy not because it is anti-technology, but because it demands a mode of attention that digital systems have made increasingly rare. Theatre requires sustained, embodied, collective focus on a single event that unfolds in real time and cannot be replayed, paused, or algorithmically optimised. Freshwater’s insistence that scholars attend to what audiences actually experience, rather than what they are assumed to feel, is particularly relevant here (Freshwater 29–33). What audiences experience in theatre is a temporary restructuring of their attentional habits a period in which the fragmented, commodified, algorithmically directed attention of daily life is replaced by something slower, deeper, and shared.

Prospero’s Epilogue, with its direct address and its request for freely given attention, may be the most powerful image this argument produces. It suggests that the real question is not whether attention can be captured  it always can but whether it can be returned. Theatre returns it. That is its political act.
